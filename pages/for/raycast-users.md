# Mirror for Raycast Users

Raycast launches your apps in 200 milliseconds. A reflex. Cmd+Space, three letters, and you're there.

Speed like that compounds. Every keystroke saved across thousands of daily interactions becomes weeks per year. That's why you use Raycast.

But here's the gap Raycast can't fill: it doesn't know *why* you're launching Figma right now, or that you're opening it because you have a design review in ten minutes with someone who raised concerns about the last iteration.

**Speed is Raycast. Purpose is Mirror.**

## The Gap: Velocity Without Direction

Raycast solves the launcher problem perfectly. You know what you want to open, and Raycast gets you there in a fraction of a second.

But consider what happens between intent and understanding:

- You launch Figma. 200ms later you're in the app. But you spend the next 10 minutes finding the right file, checking who's in the meeting, digging through email for previous feedback.
- You open Slack to "quickly check something." Raycast delivers you in milliseconds. But you don't know which of 47 unread threads matter for what you're actually doing.
- You hit Linear because something came up. Raycast launches you instantly. But you don't have the context about the person behind the ticket, or whether their previous concerns were addressed.

Raycast removes app-switching friction. It doesn't remove the cognitive friction of arriving somewhere blind.

So you're ultrafast at *getting* to your tools. But you're underprepared for *using* them.

## How Mirror Closes It: Context That Moves at Raycast's Speed

Mirror sits alongside Raycast—not replacing it, but completing it. It's a context layer that understands why you're launching what you're launching.

When you open Raycast, Mirror is already running in the background, reconciling information from your email, calendar, Slack, and notes. It builds a living knowledge graph of:

- **Who matters in your day** (by calendar proximity, recent interactions, relationship significance)
- **What you're actually working toward** (from tickets, messages, and commitments)
- **Why launches matter right now** (context about upcoming meetings, projects, and priorities)
- **What changed since last time** (new feedback, new attendees, newly resolved tensions)

The result: you don't just launch faster. You launch *purposefully*.

Imagine opening Raycast to launch Figma, and instead of launching blind, you see:

"Design review (Sarah, Mike) in 9 min. Sarah's previous concern: spacing in buttons. You've addressed it in this iteration. Mike is new to the project—intro brief included."

You launch with purpose. You know why you're there.
