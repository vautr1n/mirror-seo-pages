# Mirror for Obsidian Users

Obsidian is the thinking tool for power users. You build your second brain methodically — crafting MOCs (maps of content), linking notes, maintaining templates, creating a personal knowledge graph that evolves with your thinking.

If you use Obsidian, you already understand something fundamental: **ideas are more powerful when they're connected**. You spend hours creating bi-directional links, building relationship maps, curating your vault so that knowledge finds you when you need it.

Mirror extends this philosophy to the information you're not writing down.

## The Gap: What You Capture vs. What You Live

Obsidian power users know the cost of their tool: discipline. You have to write things down. You have to maintain links. You have to remember to update your MOC when a new connection emerges. It's worthwhile, but it's work.

Meanwhile, something important is happening outside your vault:
- Emails arrive with context that contradicts or deepens your thinking
- Meetings happen where someone connects two ideas you've been developing separately
- Calendar invites contain constraints that reshape your roadmap
- Conversations surface relationships between people and projects you haven't written down

You're building a knowledge graph manually. But you're living in a richer, messier knowledge graph that exists across your email, calendar, and conversations — and you can't see the connections.

Your Obsidian vault captures what you think *about*. It misses what you're actually *doing*.

## How Mirror Complements Obsidian

Mirror builds the same thing Obsidian trains you to build — a knowledge graph of connections between ideas — but it builds it automatically from what you already do.

No manual input. No discipline required. Just patterns emerging from your real work.

Here's what changes:

**Automatic Relationship Mapping** — You've spent months curating bi-directional links in Obsidian between related notes. Mirror does this automatically across your emails, meetings, and calendar. Someone mentions Topic A in a meeting, Topic B in an email two weeks later, and they're obviously related. Mirror connects them without you having to write anything down.

**The Graph Under Your Graph** — Your Obsidian vault is beautiful because it's intentional. Mirror reveals the *unintentional* graph — the patterns in how you actually work, who you actually talk to, what actually matters. Your vault organizes your thinking. Mirror organizes your doing. They form a layered knowledge architecture.

**Just-in-Time Context** — You open Obsidian to work on a project note. You've linked three related pages. But you don't remember that in your March 15 meeting, Sarah mentioned a constraint that's directly relevant to what you're working on now. Mirror knows. Mirror surfaces it: "Sarah raised this point in your March meeting. Your current thinking assumes you have 6 weeks, but she said the deadline shifted to Q2."

**Daily Context Without Manual Capture** — Obsidian users love daily notes. They're your thinking space. But you only capture what you remember to write down. Mirror captures what happened to you (meetings, emails, conversations) automatically — a complete daily context that's there when you open your vault.

**Connection Discovery** — You've been working on five separate projects. Your Obsidian vault treats them as separate MOCs. But Mirror notices that they all depend on the same technical platform, or all involve the same vendor, or all require buy-in from the same person. You see connections you didn't write down because they weren't obvious until you had the data.

## Before and After

**Before Mirror:**
- You open Obsidian to work on a project
- Your vault has excellent links to related thinking
- But you don't remember that a key constraint was mentioned in a meeting two months ago
- You hunt through email or calendar to find the context
- Or you work without it, and hope you didn't miss something critical
- You think about creating a note to capture that context, but it feels like overhead

**After Mirror:**
- Mirror has been automatically mapping context from your emails, meetings, and calendar
- You open Obsidian to work on the same project
- Mirror's proactive brief surfaces: "This project intersects with the vendor relationship decision (Sarah's email, March 10) and the Q2 roadmap constraint (Monday's meeting). You last discussed the budget impact in your 1-1 with James."
- You have the relational graph you need, without adding a single note to your vault
- Your thinking is enriched by context from your actual life
- When you do create a note, you're building on a foundation of automatic context, not trying to recall everything from memory

## What Doesn't Change

Your Obsidian vault remains your intentional knowledge graph. You keep building it exactly as you have been. You keep maintaining your MOCs, your templates, your linking structure.

Mirror doesn't touch that work or replace it. It simply ensures that when you're thinking in Obsidian, you have access to the automatic knowledge graph that's been building itself in your calendar, emails, and meetings.

You're not replacing one tool with another. You're layering an automatic context engine underneath the intentional thinking you're already doing.

## The Philosophy

Obsidian users understand that great thinking requires great scaffolding. You build connections because connected ideas are more useful than isolated ones.

Mirror is scaffolding too — it just scaffolds from what you actually do, not just what you intentionally write.

Your second brain will always be what you build in Obsidian. Your *context brain* — the automatic knowledge graph of what's actually happening — that's what Mirror provides.

The two together make you significantly more capable than either one alone.
