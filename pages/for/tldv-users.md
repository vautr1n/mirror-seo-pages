# Mirror for tl;dv Users

tl;dv does something powerful: it lets you record, clip, and share the moments that matter. Remote teams love it. You can pull exactly the 30-second moment that proves your point, share it with the right people, and move faster. It's become essential infrastructure for async-first teams.

But here's the thing about clipping: to know what to clip, you have to know what matters.

## The Gap: Clips Without Context

tl;dv is brilliant at capturing moments. It's not designed to tell you which moments are strategic.

You're in a meeting with a new prospect. tl;dv records beautifully. But you don't know yet: which part of this conversation is the key objection? Which moment will matter when you're talking to your CEO about deal status? What they said now versus what they said last month—is that a major shift or minor nuance? You're recording everything, but you're not sure what to clip.

After the meeting, you have a full recording and the ability to clip. But surfacing it to the right people at the right time requires you to already know the strategic narrative. Which clip matters to your sales leadership? Which one matters to your product team? What context do they need to understand why this moment is important?

tl;dv gives you the tool. You have to bring the judgment.

## How Mirror Fills the Gap

Mirror works alongside tl;dv, giving you the context intelligence to know what actually matters before you hit record—and after you do.

**Scenario 1: Smart Recording**
You're about to enter a tl;dv-recorded meeting. Mirror sends a pre-call briefing: here's what this person has raised before, here's what changed since your last conversation, here are the moments from prior conversations that shifted the relationship. You're walking into the meeting knowing exactly what to listen for. When they say something new, you recognize it as new. When they circle back to something, you know you've heard it before—and you know whether it's moving toward resolution or getting stuck.

**Scenario 2: Smart Clipping**
The meeting is recorded in tl;dv. Now Mirror goes to work. It helps you identify the moments that matter: the new objection, the shifted priority, the unmet commitment, the signal that the deal is accelerating or stalling. It surfaces the context around key moments you might want to share and with whom (your CEO needs to see commitment drift; your product team needs to hear the feature request). You're not manually searching through the recording trying to find the important bits—Mirror surfaces them.

**Scenario 3: Context for Sharing**
You're sharing a tl;dv clip with your team. Mirror packages the context alongside it: why this moment matters, what changed since the last conversation, what it means for next steps. Your team sees the 45-second clip *and* understands its strategic weight. Without that context, the clip is interesting. With it, it's actionable.

**Scenario 4: Recording as Research**
Over time, tl;dv builds your archive of recorded conversations. Mirror builds intelligence from it. It shows you: which objections keep coming up and are they getting weaker or stronger? Which people are most engaged (frequent, longer conversations) versus least engaged? When you're preparing for a new prospect, Mirror shows you the pattern of concerns across similar conversations—so you know what to listen for when tl;dv starts recording.

## Before and After

**Before Mirror:**
You record a key prospect call on tl;dv. Afterward, you have a full recording but you're not entirely sure what to pull. You watch through it, and clip three moments that feel important. You share one with your sales leadership. Two weeks later, you realize there was another moment in that recording that was actually more important—a shift in their concern that signaled they were ready to move forward. You'd recorded it. You just didn't know to clip it.

**After Mirror:**
Same prospect call, recorded on tl;dv. Before the call, Mirror briefs you: this prospect raised an implementation concern in the last meeting that we never fully addressed. Listen for whether they're still concerned about that—it's probably the moment that matters most. During the call, they do address it. Afterward, Mirror watches the recording and flags exactly that moment: "This is the clip that matters. This person is now confident about implementation. Share it with sales leadership; it changes deal momentum." You clip it, contextualize it, and send it to your leadership with Mirror's summary of why it matters. Within an hour, leadership has repositioned the deal. You recorded the right thing; Mirror made sure you clipped the right thing.

## What Doesn't Change

You keep tl;dv. You keep recording, clipping, and sharing exactly as you do now. Mirror sits alongside it, adding the context intelligence that helps you know what to listen for and what to clip. tl;dv handles recording and sharing. Mirror handles the judgment about what matters.

This isn't about adding features to tl;dv. It's about giving you the strategic context to make tl;dv more powerful.
