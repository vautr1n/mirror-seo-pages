# Twenty Minutes Before a Sales Call: Context Reconstruction Panic

Your calendar pings. Sales call in 20 minutes.

You scramble:

1. Open your CRM. Search the prospect's name.
2. Skim their company profile (you've read it three times).
3. Check the deal stage. "Did I send the proposal?"
4. Scroll through past call notes—they're sparse, vague.
5. Jump to email. Find the thread.
6. Look at Slack. Are there any updates?
7. Join the call 2 minutes late, still not fully prepared.

You close some deals. But how many slip because you walked in underprepared? How many did you lose because you missed a detail?

## The Problem: Sales Is a Memory Game Without Perfect Memory

Sales success isn't about how good your product is. It's about how much you remember.

You need to know:

- **Their history with you:** How many calls? What did they say they needed? What changed?
- **The real timeline:** When do they actually need to decide? Have they pushed deadlines before?
- **Open objections:** What concerns came up last time? Did you actually address them? Or did they just stop mentioning them?
- **The gap:** What did you promise they'd get? What's actually possible? Where might this break down?
- **Who's actually deciding:** The person you're talking to—are they the real decision-maker? What do *they* care about vs. what the org cares about?
- **Relationship temperature:** Are they warming up or cooling down? Do they trust you? How much?
- **Competition:** Who else are they looking at? Where are you winning? Where are you weak?

Most CRMs have pieces of this. But it's fragmented. Call notes from a month ago that are sparse. A deal stage that might be wrong. An email thread that only shows one part of the conversation.

So 20 minutes before the call, you're reconstructing, not remembering.

## Why Your CRM Alone Isn't Enough

Your CRM is a database. It stores information. But it doesn't *synthesize* information.

You can have:
- 5 call notes
- 12 emails
- 2 proposal versions
- Historical notes
- Deal history

But you have to manually piece them together into a coherent story. "OK, so they originally needed X, but on the last call they mentioned Y was more important, and they were concerned about Z..."

This is why sales teams end up with:
- **Lost deals:** You didn't realize the deal was slipping until it was too late
- **Missed objections:** You didn't know the real concern was never addressed
- **Inconsistent messaging:** You promised something the last rep said differently
- **Low trust:** Prospect feels like you're learning about them for the first time every call

The CRM isn't the problem. The *synthesis* is the problem.

## How Mirror Solves It

Mirror synthesizes your sales history into a deal briefing.

**For every prospect, Mirror gives you:**

1. **Deal Story:** Not just "where are we," but "how did we get here and where are we headed?"
   - Original problem they were solving
   - How their needs have evolved
   - Your conversations over time
   - Key decisions and commitments
   - What's changed in their world

2. **Prospect Profile:** Who you're talking to and why they matter
   - Their role and what they care about
   - Their communication style
   - Their history with similar decisions
   - Their relationship temperature with you
   - Their concerns (explicit and implicit)

3. **Deal Intelligence:** What's actually happening beneath the surface
   - Timeline (real timeline, not stated timeline)
   - Decision criteria (what actually matters to them)
   - Competitors (who you're up against)
   - Budget reality (can they actually afford this)
   - Risks (where this deal could break down)

4. **Your Playbook:** Specific recommendations for *this* conversation
   - What to ask about
   - What not to assume
   - Previous objections and how you've addressed them
   - Recommended talking points for their situation
   - What success looks like for them

5. **Relationship Context:** How you work together
   - Your communication patterns
   - What's worked before
   - Previous misalignments
   - Relationship trust level
   - Next steps (what you said you'd do)
