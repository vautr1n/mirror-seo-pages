# 400 Context Switches Per Day. Zero Signal Preservation.

You context switch constantly. The research says 400 times a day if you're a knowledge worker.

You know this kills productivity. "It takes 23 minutes to regain focus." "Switching costs." We all know the clichés.

But the real cost isn't lost time. It's something worse: *signal loss*.

## The Real Problem: Signal Fragments Across Tools

Everyone talks about context switching as a time problem. "I lost 10 minutes refocusing."

But that's not the expensive part.

The expensive part is what you *don't see*: signal that should compound, fragmenting instead.

Here's the actual pattern:

**9:15am:** Design review. Team flags a UX concern with your component library. You note it. You're going to fix it.

**10:15am:** Email from a customer asking for a feature. The request is vague. You file it mentally.

**11am:** Product meeting. Someone asks: "Should we be doing that feature the customer mentioned?" You vaguely remember the email but not the details. You defer.

**1pm:** You start fixing the UX issue from this morning.

**2pm:** Email from the customer again. They need the feature by Q2 for their launch. They're frustrated you haven't responded.

**3pm:** Slack question: "Are we shipping that feature?" Now you realize the customer said it's for Q2. But you don't remember what the feature actually was. The UX concern. The timeline pressure. Whether it's possible.

Here's what happened:

The same signal came at you three times, through three channels, at different moments. But because you context-switched between tools, you never integrated them. You treated each mention as separate information instead of the same conversation.

Result: A decision made with 40% of the information you actually had. A commitment to the customer that might be impossible. A team frustrated by unclear priorities.

The cost isn't time. It's decision quality.

## Why Current Tools Can't Solve This

You've tried to fight this:

- **Keeping notes:** But you forget to look at them
- **Tagging things:** But you don't remember the tags when you need them
- **Using a single tool for everything:** But everything doesn't fit in one tool
- **Blocking focus time:** But your job doesn't work in blocked focus time — it's reactive

The problem isn't *how* you work. The problem is that your tools are fragmented.

Your email doesn't know about your Slack. Your notes don't know about your calendar. Your Slack doesn't surface what you decided in a meeting yesterday.

So when you context switch, you don't just lose time. You lose *connection* between pieces of information.

## How Mirror Closes the Gap

Mirror reconciles context across all your tools.

**Here's what this means:**

When you're in a meeting and someone mentions "that feature request," Mirror surfaces:
- When you first heard about it (email, Slack, or previous meeting)
- Who brought it up originally
- What the customer's timeline is
- What you committed to
- What's changed since then
- How it relates to other work

You don't have to remember it. You don't have to switch to email to find it. You don't have to dig through notes.

Mirror has reconciled all the signals across all your tools. The information is *there*, in the context you need it.

## Example: The Real Cost of Context Switching

**Without Mirror:**

**Monday 11am: Product meeting**
- Someone mentions: "Should we do the integration feature?"
- You vaguely remember a customer asked about it
- You say: "Yeah, someone wanted that. I'll look into it."
- Decision: Deferred. No resolution.

**Monday 2pm: You check email**
- Email from customer: "I mentioned the integration feature last week. We need it by April 1st for our Q2 launch."
- You think: "Wait, that's two weeks away. That's urgent."
- But the information came as an *email*, not connected to the *meeting*
- You reply: "We'll prioritize it" — without context on what else you're committed to

**Monday 4pm: You're planning the sprint**
- You realize you committed to the integration feature
- But you don't remember: Is it actually possible in two weeks? Does it require architecture changes? What else is due April 1st?
- You check your calendar. You have customer meetings and a conference talk.
- You commit to something you might not be able to deliver

**Tuesday: Reality hits**
- You start the feature. It requires architecture changes.
- You realize you can't deliver in two weeks.
- You have to back-track, apologize, reschedule

The cost: Trust with the customer, team morale, your credibility.

What went wrong: You didn't connect the signals. You treated the meeting mention and the email mention as separate information instead of the same conversation happening in two channels.

**With Mirror:**

**Monday 11am: Product meeting**
- Someone mentions: "Should we do the integration feature?"
- Mirror surfaces: "Customer TechCorp mentioned this feature on Friday. They need it by April 1st. The last architecture review flagged this as requiring core changes."
- Decision made with full context: "Yes, but we need to start today and evaluate architecture impact."

**Monday 2pm: Email from customer arrives**
- Customer: "Just following up on the integration feature. We need it by April 1st for Q2 launch."
- You reply: "We're prioritizing it starting tomorrow. Architecture review happening this week, ship target April 1st."
- The customer gets a response that shows you understood the urgency and timeline

**Monday 4pm: Sprint planning**
- You already have full context. You know the feature is prioritized. You know the architecture complexity. You know the deadline.
- You plan correctly. You commit to something you can deliver.

**Tuesday: Execution**
- You start the architecture review knowing exactly what you're solving for
- You deliver on time
- Customer trust increases

The difference: Signal wasn't lost in context switching. Every mention of the feature was connected. You weren't learning the same information three times.
