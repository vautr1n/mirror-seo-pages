# Mirror for Founders: Your AI That Actually Knows Your Company

## Your Day Without Mirror

6am: Board deck review. Did you hit last quarter's metrics? What question did Sarah ask about churn that you said you'd investigate? Your slides say one thing. Your memory says another.

8am: Investor call. Ten VCs competing for attention. This one—did they say they lead or follow? What's their thesis? What did you promise to send them? You have 90 seconds before the call.

9am: Standup with your eight direct reports. Each one needs context you've only partially remembered. One carried a project from last quarter that's now stalled. Another is fighting a resource constraint you said you'd solve. The third is waiting on feedback you gave in Slack three weeks ago.

10am: Customer renewal call. $500k. They're thinking about shopping around. What was the first thing they complained about when they signed? What have they asked for that you haven't built? What's the sentiment trend across your last five conversations?

11am: Hiring debrief. A candidate who interviewed yesterday reminds you of someone. What did your team say about the last two hires in that profile? What's your bar?

Lunch with your advisor. They gave you three pieces of advice last month. Which ones did you actually do?

Afternoon: Firefighting between fundraising, product, hiring, and operations. Each context switch costs you 15 minutes to reconstruct what you already know.

You are the most context-rich person in your company. You are also the most context-fragmented.

---

## The Problem

Founder context is not static. It's relational, dynamic, and scattered.

Your investors have fund theses, investment patterns, decision timelines, and a conversation history with you. Your board members have specific concerns and commitments they've made. Your advisors have given advice. Your team knows their work; you need to know them and their commitments. Your customers have problems, sentiment arcs, and renewal dates. Your candidates have backgrounds and feedback waiting to be synthesized.

This context lives in Gmail. In Slack threads from four months ago. In Google Drive folders you can't quite remember. In calendar invites with notes buried in descriptions. In Notion docs someone created but you're not sure who maintains. In your own memory, which is excellent and also full.

A founder spends roughly 30% of their cognitive load on context reconstruction. You're smart enough to build a company. You shouldn't also be a full-time archaeologist of your own relationships.

## What Mirror Does for Founders

Mirror is your personal AI context layer. It learns from your email, Slack, calendar, and documents. It builds a context graph of your relationships, commitments, and company dynamics. And before every meeting, it briefs you.

### Board Prep

Before your next board meeting, Mirror surfaces every metric commitment you made, every question directors asked that you said you'd address, every metric you reported and the trajectory. Not from your deck. From your actual emails and Slack threads. Sarah asked about churn in July. It's October. Here's what you said you'd do about it. Here's what's changed.

### Investor Calls

New investor wants to talk. Mirror knows: their fund thesis, the last three points you discussed with them, what you said you'd send them and whether you sent it, what's changed in your metrics since you last talked, which of their portfolio companies overlap with your customer base, the tone of the conversation. You have a full brief 90 seconds before the call.

### 1:1s with Direct Reports

You have a standup with your VP of Engineering. Mirror knows: what she's working on, what she raised in your last three 1:1s, which open requests are waiting on you, which ones are waiting on her team, the sentiment trend. You walk in prepared. The conversation goes deeper because you're not reconstructing basics.

### Customer Relationships

Renewal conversation in two days. $500k ARR. Mirror traces the full history: first call, tone of that conversation, every escalation, every feature request and whether you built it, every complaint and whether you fixed it, current sentiment. You know exactly which commitments to address.

### Hiring

Candidate is coming in for the final round. Mirror has the recruiter notes, their background, what your team said about the last three similar profiles, your hiring bar for the role. Your team walks in knowing who they're talking to.

### Advisor Meetings

Your advisor gave you three pieces of advice in September. Mirror remembers which ones you acted on, which ones you decided against, which ones are in motion. You don't waste their time repeating yourself.

## The Math

You average 30+ meetings per week. Assume 15 minutes of context reconstruction per meeting on average. That's 7.5 hours per week—375 hours per year—of cognitive load that isn't thinking, it's remembering.

Mirror reduces context reconstruction to 90 seconds per meeting. 45 minutes per week. 39 hours per year.

But the real number is the one you never measure: the number of times you don't walk into a meeting cold, the number of commitments you don't forget, the number of relationship signals you don't miss because you're too busy context-switching, the decisions you make faster because you have information instead of opinion.
