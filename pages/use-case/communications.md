# Mirror for Communications Officers

The journalist calls. They're writing about your company's position on labor practices in your supply chain. What did your CEO say about this six months ago? What did the press release actually say? What was the internal Slack discussion when you first discovered the issue? Did anyone promise exclusivity to another outlet? Your Chief Communications Officer is in a board meeting. You have 20 minutes to respond. The context exists. It's scattered across email threads, five press releases, three Slack channels, meeting notes from October, and the head of someone who just gave notice.

This is the reality of institutional communications. Every statement must be consistent with everything said before — to media, investors, employees, regulators, partners. One contradictory quote and you have a crisis on your hands. But the institutional memory of what's been communicated doesn't live in one place. It lives nowhere. And when the spokesperson leaves, half of it disappears.

## The Problem

Communications is the ultimate context-dependent function. You speak for the company. That means you need to know:

- What the CEO said about this issue two years ago, and why they said it that way
- What the board was told in the confidential deck
- What's public, what's off-the-record, what was promised exclusive
- Which journalists have been cultivated as allies, which have been adversarial
- Which investors were nervous about this topic, and what reassurance they were given
- What employees have been told, and what the gap is between external and internal messaging
- The timeline of how this issue unfolded across every channel

Get one detail wrong and you create inconsistency. Get inconsistency to media and you create a story that contradicts the company's stated position. That becomes the story. Not your policy. The contradiction.

The context exists. It's just fragmented across eight different tools, conversations no one wrote down, and people's memory.

## What Mirror Does for Communications Professionals

**Media prep.** Before a journalist call, you need to know everything. Mirror surfaces the complete relationship history with this reporter: what they've written about you before, what you've told them, what you've promised on background, what's on the record from your side. The last three times your CEO spoke to them. Whether they've been asked for exclusivity. When they call, you're not starting from zero.

**Message consistency.** Mirror traces every public and private statement on a given topic — across press releases, CEO quotes, internal talking points, investor presentations, board materials, Slack. Before you draft anything new, you see everything you've already said. You see the exact language used. You see who was in the room. You see what was conditional or time-bound. Consistency stops being luck.

**Crisis response.** Something breaks. A regulatory filing. An employee lawsuit. A news story. You need to know instantly: who inside the company knows this? What have we already said publicly? What have we said privately to the board? What have we promised to regulators? What's the timeline of events across all communication channels. Mirror shows you the full picture in minutes, not hours.

**Stakeholder mapping.** Your board has twelve members. You have relationships with 40+ institutional investors. You track 15 key journalist relationships. You work with three analyst firms. Each stakeholder group has heard a different version of your story — not because you lied, but because different contexts require different emphasis. Mirror maps every stakeholder relationship and the communication history within it.

**Institutional memory.** Your VP of External Relations takes a role at a competitor. Your PR director gets recruited to a bigger company. When they leave, what leaves with them? In most organizations: relationship context, promise-keeping history, tribal knowledge about what was said when, and why. Mirror keeps that in the context graph. The relationships stay with the company.

## FAQ

**Q: Won't this create compliance risk if everything is tracked?**
A: The opposite. Mirror creates the documentation trail that regulators actually want to see. You can prove consistency. You can show that stakeholder commitments were tracked and kept. You can demonstrate that your institutional voice was intentional, not reactive.

**Q: What if we need to delete or revise messaging history?**
A: Mirror tracks revisions. You can see what was said, what changed, when, and why. This is valuable in a crisis — it shows you were updating based on new information, not shifting position arbitrarily. That's defensible.

**Q: How does this work with press embargo management?**
A: Mirror tracks embargo dates and conditions for every journalist relationship. Before you talk to a reporter about an upcoming announcement, Mirror reminds you of any prior commitments about exclusivity windows.

**Q: Can our team collaborate on this, or is this just for the Chief Communications Officer?**
A: Designed for teams. Your PR lead, your investor relations person, your internal communications manager, your executive comms specialists — they all need institutional memory. Mirror surfaces different relevant context for different roles.
